#function to create five minute weighted windows
def weighted_fiveMinInt(quantityTS,priceTS,tsOfIntervals):
    weights = quantityTS*priceTS
    fiveMinIntervals = np.zeros(len(tsOfIntervals)-1)
    for i in range(len(tsOfIntervals)-1):
        weightSlice = sum(weights[tsOfIntervals[i]:tsOfIntervals[i+1]])
        quantSlice = sum(quantityTS[tsOfIntervals[i]:tsOfIntervals[i+1]])
        newPrice = weightSlice/quantSlice
        fiveMinIntervals[i] = newPrice
    return fiveMinIntervals

For starters, we are treating the errors in this data as iid ~N(0,simga^2).

The predicition is the last value of the first order exponential smoother (forecast origin). This is because we are treating the data as a constant process. Each subsequent prediction will also be the last value of the first order exponential smoother.

The prediciton intervals are generated by adding and subtracting a margin of error from the forcast origin, where the MOE is the product a two-sided critical z-score (alpha/2) & the estimated standard deviation of the errors.

We're still assuming that the errors are iid~N(0,sigma^2).

The predicition has changed from the previous problem because we are now accounting for a trend in the data. Adding the trend term to the smoother enables us to achieve forecast values that differ from the forecast origin. In this particular case, the negative trend in the data appears to have influenced a negative trend in the predicted values.

The predicition interval is now generated by adding the trend to the forecast origin value and then adding and subtracting the MOE. It's important to note that the trend value here is a function of the forecast step. For each step beyond the forecast origin, the trend value changes. Additionally, the MOE error term is now the product of the MOE term described in 1d and a value that grows with each forecast step. So, now the predicition intervals will adjust the trend for subsequent forecast steps and the MOE will grow indefinitely.

The optimal value is a much closer fit for the data than part a, however, these optimal values greatly change the predicition interval. The lower gammma value initially creates a smaller PI, which doesn't last since gamma grows with each forecast step. Since the prediction interval has the product of alpha^2 and gamma in the MOE, the higher alpha value from the optimization causes the prediction intervals to grow much faster.

Like the previous problems, this prediction is based on the forecast origin. In this particular forecast, we are now adding the trend and the seasonal component. Since the seasonal component is a dominant cycle observed in the data, it enables the predictions to utilize the observed cycle to make a better predicition.

The prediction intervals, from what I can tell between the book and the internet are the same as the Holt's Method PI's. The PI should continue to grow with each forecast step and larger values of gamma/alpha will cause the PI's to be wider (initially) and grow faster (than the growth observed in the data above).

The Durbin-Watson test looks for autocorrelation at lag one.
Ho: No Serial Autocorrelation (at lag 1)
Ha: Serial Autocorrelation
According to the text, for uncorrelated values the Durbin-Watson test statistic should be approximately 2. With a Durbin-Watson test statistic of 2.06 we fail to reject the Null Hypothesis. Therefore, we have no evidence of serial autocorrelation (at one lag) in our data.

The LBQ Test looks for autocorrelation in the the lags from 1 to p. In this case, p = 75.
Ho: No Serial Autocorrelation (from lags 1 to 75)
Ha: Serial Autocorrelation
With a LBQ Test statistics of 70.028 and a p-value of .6408 (using a Chi-square distribution with 75 degrees of freedom) we fail to reject the Null Hypothesis. Therefore, we have no evidence of serial autocorrelation (from lag 1 to 75) in our data.
